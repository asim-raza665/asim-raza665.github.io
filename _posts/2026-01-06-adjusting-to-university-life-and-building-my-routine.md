### Facing the Academic Shift and Early Struggles

After experiencing my first day at university, the real challenge began, adjusting to a completely new academic and social environment. The first week of classes was a mixture of confusion, curiosity, and pressure. Everything felt very different from my FSc life, where studies were more structured, guided, and predictable. In university, I had to manage everything on my own, including attending lectures, completing assignments, handling hostel life, and doing self-study. This sudden shift in responsibility made the initial days quite challenging for me.

One of the biggest challenges I faced was the subject of Programming Fundamentals. Coming from an FSc Pre-Engineering background, and even earlier from a biology-focused matric, I had no exposure to computers or programming at all. While subjects like Calculus, Applied Physics, and Discrete Mathematics were somewhat familiar and easier to understand, programming was completely new and unfamiliar for me. In our first lectures, we started learning Python, beginning with very basic concepts such as variables. For many students, these concepts seemed simple and easy to grasp, but for me, everything felt new and difficult to understand. I had to put in extra effort just to follow what was being taught in class.

The gap between my background and what was being taught became more obvious with every passing day. My classmates who had studied computer science in their intermediate or matric years seemed comfortable from the very beginning. They could write small programs quickly, debug errors without much confusion, and follow the teacher's explanations without needing to pause and look things up. I, on the other hand, was still trying to understand what a variable actually meant in a program and why it behaved differently from a mathematical variable I had used in Calculus. These small confusions added up quickly, and by the end of the first week, I had a long list of concepts I had heard but not truly understood.

What made this harder was the pace of university lectures. Teachers did not slow down to check whether every student had understood before moving to the next topic. The expectation was that students would identify their own gaps and fill them independently. This was a completely new experience for me because in FSc, teachers would often repeat explanations and check comprehension regularly. In university, I had to become responsible for my own learning, and in those early days, I was not yet equipped for that level of independence. I often left lectures with more questions than I had arrived with, and figuring out how to answer those questions on my own was one of the earliest and most important lessons of university life.

### Language Barrier, Self-Learning, and Academic Pressure

Another major difficulty I faced was the language barrier. All lectures were delivered in English, and in the beginning, I struggled to fully understand the explanations given by the teachers. Sometimes, I could not grasp the concepts properly during the lecture, which made it harder for me to keep up with the class. To overcome this issue, I started using online tools like ChatGPT and other learning resources to understand concepts in Urdu. I also explored different YouTube channels and online lectures to strengthen my understanding. Even after putting in this effort, the first couple of weeks remained quite stressful. I often felt confused and unsure about how to study effectively in this new academic system.

University life also introduced a completely new academic structure, including weekly assignments, quizzes, and continuous assessment. This was very different from the annual board exam system I was used to. Initially, I found it difficult to manage my time properly. In fact, for the first two weeks, I struggled to even begin studying seriously due to stress, confusion, and overthinking. I was trying to understand how everything worked while also dealing with the pressure of keeping up with my studies.

The quiz system was particularly difficult to adjust to in those early weeks. Unlike board exams where the entire syllabus is announced in advance and students have months to prepare, university quizzes could come with very short notice and cover recent lectures directly. Missing a concept in one class could mean performing poorly in a quiz the very next week. I experienced this pressure firsthand during the first few quizzes when I realized that my preparation was not matching the level expected. Those early results were disappointing, but they were also honest feedback. They showed me exactly where I was falling behind and gave me something concrete to fix.

The assignments in Programming Fundamentals added another layer of pressure. Each assignment required logical thinking and problem-solving that went beyond simply reading notes and reproducing answers. I had to actually write code that worked, and in the beginning, writing even a simple program felt like an enormous task. I would spend a long time on problems that my classmates finished quickly, and that comparison made me feel more behind than I probably was. But I kept trying, because giving up was never an option I seriously considered. Every time I managed to fix an error or get a program to produce the correct output, I felt a small but real sense of progress. Those small victories kept me going through the most difficult weeks.

Managing hostel life alongside academic pressure also required adjustment. In FSc, I had lived at home where meals, laundry, and daily routines were handled without much thought. In the hostel, everything became my own responsibility. Waking up on time, managing meals, keeping my belongings organized, and finding time to study in a shared environment were all new challenges. Some days I handled them well, and other days the logistics of hostel life ate into the time I had planned for studying. Finding the right balance took time, and during the first few weeks, I was still figuring out how to manage my energy across all of these demands at once.

### Developing Routine, Support System, and Academic Improvement

However, things gradually started to improve after two to three weeks. I began to develop a proper routine for myself. I started attending classes more actively, paying closer attention during lectures, and focusing on understanding concepts rather than just completing assignments. During this time, I also met some very supportive seniors who guided me and reassured me that this difficult phase was temporary and that things would become easier with time.

One of the most important turning points in my journey was meeting my friend, Tahir Yaqoob from Sargodha (Bhalwal). He was an intelligent and hardworking student who later became one of the top performers in our class. His support and guidance helped me a lot, especially in understanding difficult concepts and improving my communication skills. He explained things in a simple way, which made learning easier for me. Over time, he became more than just a classmate; he became a true and supportive friend.

Having Tahir as a study companion changed the way I approached difficult topics. When I struggled with a concept in Programming Fundamentals, I could discuss it with him and he would break it down in a way that made sense to me. We would sometimes sit together after dinner in the hostel and go through problems from assignments or upcoming quiz topics. These sessions were more effective than studying alone because explaining and discussing ideas out loud helped both of us understand them more deeply. I noticed that my confidence grew steadily during these sessions, and by the time I walked into a quiz or a lecture, I felt more prepared than I had during those first confusing weeks.

The seniors I met during this period also played an important role. They had gone through the same adjustment period themselves and understood exactly what I was feeling. They told me which study habits worked, which resources were most useful, and how to manage the balance between academics and hostel life. Their reassurance that these early struggles were normal and temporary helped me stop catastrophizing my situation and start focusing on practical improvement instead. Knowing that others had passed through the same difficulty and come out stronger on the other side made the challenge feel more manageable.

As my routine became more stable, my performance in quizzes and assignments started to reflect the effort I was putting in. Concepts that had once confused me began to make sense. Python, which had felt completely foreign in the first week, started to feel like a language I was slowly learning to speak. I still made mistakes and still had questions, but I was no longer drowning in confusion. I was learning how to swim. That shift in feeling, from overwhelmed to capable, was one of the most significant experiences of my entire first semester.
