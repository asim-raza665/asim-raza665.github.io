### Entering Hostel Life and Initial Expectations vs Reality

After securing my admission to UET Lahore (Faisalabad Campus), a completely new chapter of my life began: my first experience of hostel life. Before joining university, I had never lived in a hostel or stayed away from home for such a long period. Because of this, everything about this transition felt unfamiliar and somewhat overwhelming. I was excited because I was starting my university journey, but at the same time, I was nervous about leaving the comfort of my home and adapting to a completely new environment.

Like many students, I had my own expectations about hostel life. Based on stories from friends, social media posts, and my imagination, I expected hostel life to be a mixture of independence, fun, and personal growth. I thought I would be assigned a room with only a few students where everyone would have their own space and a comfortable environment for studying and relaxing. However, reality turned out to be quite different from what I had imagined.

Before the start of the semester, the university administration announced that hostel rooms would be allotted on a "first come, first serve" basis. At that time, I did not pay much attention to this announcement. I assumed that there would be plenty of accommodation available and that I would easily get a room in one of the better hostels. Because of this assumption, I was not in a hurry to arrive at the campus.

When I finally reached the university and began the hostel allocation process, I quickly realized my mistake. The most popular hostels, Al-Biruni Hall and Ali Hall, had already been filled by students who arrived earlier. Since these hostels were considered among the better accommodation options, many students had secured their places as soon as possible. Unfortunately, by the time I arrived, there were no vacancies left.

As a result, I was assigned to Umar Hall, which was the only available option. Initially, I was disappointed because it was very different from the image I had created in my mind. Instead of small rooms with a few students, Umar Hall consisted of large shared halls where many students lived together. Some halls accommodated between 10 and 24 students at the same time. I was assigned to a hall with approximately 12 students.

When I first entered the hall, I felt surprised. Seeing so many students living in one large space was something completely new for me. Back at home, I had always enjoyed privacy and a familiar environment. Suddenly, I found myself surrounded by strangers from different cities and backgrounds. It was a major change, and I was unsure about how I would adjust.

That first day taught me an important lesson: planning and timing matter. Had I arrived earlier, I might have secured accommodation in one of the hostels I originally wanted. Although I could not change the situation, I knew I had to accept it and make the best of the opportunity available to me.

### Struggles of Adjustment and Living Conditions in the Hostel

The first few days in the hostel were not easy. Apart from adjusting to the large shared hall, I also faced challenges related to basic facilities. During the admission period, there was a shortage of beds because a large number of new students had arrived at the same time. For a while, arranging a proper sleeping space became an additional concern.

Everything felt unfamiliar. The environment was different, the daily routine was different, and even simple activities required adjustment. At home, many things were organized and predictable. In the hostel, I had to learn how to manage things on my own. Initially, this transition felt difficult because I was stepping far outside my comfort zone.

The shared hall environment also required adaptation. With around twelve students sharing the same space, privacy was limited. Students had different schedules, habits, and personalities. Some preferred studying late at night, while others liked sleeping early. Some enjoyed talking and socializing, while others preferred quiet environments. Learning how to coexist with people from different backgrounds was one of the first challenges of hostel life.

During the first few days, most of us were strangers to one another. Conversations were limited, and everyone seemed busy settling into their new surroundings. However, as time passed, small interactions gradually turned into meaningful conversations. We started introducing ourselves, discussing our hometowns, and sharing stories about our admission journeys.

One of the most common topics of discussion was entry test preparation. Many students shared their experiences of appearing in NET, ECAT, and other university entrance examinations. Listening to their stories was interesting because everyone had faced different challenges before reaching university. Some students had traveled from distant cities, while others had overcome financial or personal difficulties to continue their education.

These conversations helped break the initial awkwardness. Gradually, the hall began to feel less like a place filled with strangers and more like a community of students working toward similar goals. Although we came from different backgrounds, we shared the common experience of starting university life.

Living in the hostel also taught me practical life skills. I learned how to organize my belongings, manage my daily routine, and take responsibility for tasks that had previously been handled by family members. These responsibilities may seem small, but they played an important role in developing independence and self-discipline.

Another challenge was adjusting to the hostel environment itself. Noise levels, crowded spaces, and the lack of privacy sometimes made studying difficult. However, over time, I learned how to adapt and focus despite these distractions. This ability to adjust became one of the most valuable lessons of my hostel experience.

### Gradual Adaptation, Friendships, and Learning Independence

Although my hostel journey started with discomfort and uncertainty, things gradually improved. As I spent more time with my fellow residents, friendships began to develop naturally. The students who initially seemed like strangers slowly became familiar faces and eventually close friends.

One particularly memorable moment was the evening before our first university classes. Since classes were starting the next day, everyone was busy preparing for this important occasion. Students ironed their clothes, polished their shoes, organized their books, and discussed their class schedules. There was a sense of excitement throughout the hall because we were all about to begin a new chapter of our lives.

That night, conversations continued for hours. Some students talked about their expectations from university, while others discussed their future career goals. Although we came from different places, we all shared similar hopes and ambitions. These discussions helped strengthen our friendships and created a sense of belonging.

Around the same time, we were introduced to the hostel mess system. We received meal tokens and learned how the dining arrangements worked. This was another completely new experience for me because life in the hostel operated very differently from life at home. Simple activities such as meal timings, attendance, and daily routines followed structured systems that required adjustment.

As weeks passed, the environment that once felt uncomfortable gradually became familiar. I started recognizing faces across the hostel, understanding the daily routine, and feeling more confident in my ability to manage life independently. The challenges that initially seemed difficult became normal parts of everyday life.

The friendships formed during those early days became one of the most valuable aspects of my hostel experience. Whether it was helping each other with assignments, sharing notes, discussing academic problems, or simply spending time together, these friendships made hostel life enjoyable and meaningful. Living together created bonds that extended beyond the classroom and helped build a strong support system.

Looking back now, I realize that hostel life was about much more than accommodation. It was a training ground for independence, responsibility, and personal growth. It taught me how to adapt to unfamiliar situations, communicate effectively with different people, and manage my own affairs without relying on family support.

Although my first impression of hostel life was not entirely positive, the experience ultimately became one of the most important parts of my university journey. The challenges taught me resilience, the friendships brought happiness, and the responsibilities helped me mature. Most importantly, hostel life prepared me for future challenges by teaching me that growth often begins when we step outside our comfort zones.

Today, when I look back at those early days in Umar Hall, I no longer remember the disappointment of not getting my preferred hostel. Instead, I remember the lessons I learned, the friendships I built, and the experiences that helped shape me into a more confident and independent person. What initially seemed like an unfortunate situation eventually became one of the most valuable learning experiences of my university life.
