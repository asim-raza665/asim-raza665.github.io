### Early Confusion About Degree and Future Concerns

During the early weeks of my university life, one of the most common concerns among students in our class was about our degree program. We were enrolled in BSc Computer Engineering under SEC instead of PEC, and this created a lot of confusion and uncertainty. Many students, including myself, started to question whether we were in the right field. There were discussions in classrooms, hostels, and even during breaks about future job opportunities, the value of our degree, and whether not being registered under PEC would limit our careers. This situation created stress and doubt in our minds, especially because we had just started our university journey and were already worried about our future.

Since we were first-semester students, most of us had very little knowledge about how the technology industry actually works. We often heard different opinions from relatives, friends, seniors, and social media. Some people would say that a particular degree title was extremely important, while others argued that companies only cared about skills. Because we were new to university life, it was difficult to determine which opinion was correct. Every conversation seemed to create another question instead of providing an answer.

I remember that many students would discuss these concerns before classes started or while walking back to the hostel. Some students worried that they had selected the wrong field. Others worried that future employers would judge them based on the degree title alone. As a result, many of us spent valuable time thinking about issues that were beyond our control. Instead of focusing completely on learning and adapting to university life, we often found ourselves discussing uncertainty about the future.

For me personally, the situation felt even more challenging because I came from a non-computer background. University life itself was already a major adjustment. New subjects, new teaching methods, new classmates, and new responsibilities required significant effort. At the same time, Programming Fundamentals was introducing concepts that were completely new to me. While trying to understand programming logic, I was also hearing concerns about the future value of our degree. Naturally, this created confusion.

Looking back now, I realize that these concerns are common among first-year students. When people enter university, they often focus too much on labels and too little on the actual learning process. However, at that time, we lacked the experience needed to understand the bigger picture. We wanted guidance from someone who understood both academics and the technology field. Fortunately, that guidance came at the right moment.

### Guidance and Mindset Shift by Dr. Bilal Ahmad (PF Teacher)

At that time, our Programming Fundamentals teacher, **Dr. Bilal Ahmad**, played a very important role in guiding us. He was not only a knowledgeable and intelligent teacher but also someone who understood the mindset of students. One day, he addressed this issue in class when he realized that many students were confused and worried. Instead of ignoring our concerns, he openly discussed them and gave us a very clear and practical perspective.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad explained that in fields like computer engineering and software-related domains, success does not depend solely on the title of “engineer” or registration with a particular council. He emphasized that the real value lies in skills. According to him, industries usually focus on what a person can do, what problems they can solve, how they think, and how effectively they can apply their knowledge. This perspective was different from many assumptions students had when they first entered university.

He encouraged us to focus on learning programming, improving problem-solving abilities, and building practical skills. Rather than spending time worrying about labels, he advised us to invest that energy in learning technologies and strengthening our technical foundation. His message was simple but powerful. It helped many students realize that our future would be influenced more by our abilities than by our assumptions.

One statement from that discussion remained in my mind:

> "Your skills will define your future, not just your degree title."

That single idea changed the way many of us viewed our education. Instead of constantly comparing ourselves with students from other programs, we began focusing more on our own growth. We started paying attention to the things we could control, such as learning, practice, discipline, and improvement.

During our Programming Fundamentals course, Dr. Bilal Ahmad often encouraged students to think beyond marks and focus on real problem-solving. His discussions were not limited to syntax or programming exercises. He frequently connected classroom concepts with real-world applications. Because of his background in AI, Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning (DL), he often highlighted how programming serves as a foundation for many advanced technologies.

He also discussed the importance of high-quality data in modern computing applications. One point that I remember clearly was his emphasis on data quality and accuracy. He often mentioned that machine learning models are only as useful as the data used to train them. For this reason, he frequently referred to medical datasets as examples of carefully collected data because they are usually gathered with a high level of precision and accuracy. Even though we were studying basic programming concepts at the time, these discussions helped us understand how foundational skills could eventually contribute to solving meaningful real-world problems.

Another thing I appreciated about his teaching style was that he encouraged understanding rather than memorization. He often reminded students that programming is not about remembering code line by line. Instead, it is about understanding logic and developing the ability to solve problems systematically. This approach helped me understand why strong problem-solving skills are so important in computing fields.

As my understanding of computing started to grow, I became curious about the broader areas connected to programming and technology. Out of interest, I explored some of the professional work shared by Dr. Bilal Ahmad through his LinkedIn profile. It gave me a clearer picture of how the concepts we were learning in Programming Fundamentals could eventually connect with research, Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and real-world problem solving. Seeing those connections made me more motivated to take my learning seriously and focus on building practical skills rather than worrying about labels.

### Personal Impact and Long-Term Learning

For me personally, this discussion was very motivating. At a time when I was already struggling with Programming Fundamentals due to my non-computer background, his guidance gave me confidence. I realized that instead of being stressed about things beyond my control, I should invest my energy in improving my skills. This advice helped me stay focused and work harder in my studies.

After that discussion, I started looking at my academic journey differently. Whenever I faced difficulty understanding a programming concept, I tried to focus on learning rather than becoming discouraged. Instead of comparing myself with classmates who already had programming experience, I concentrated on making gradual improvements. This mindset reduced stress and made learning more enjoyable.

As the semester progressed, I noticed that confidence grows through practice. Every solved problem, completed assignment, and successful program contributed to my development. Improvement did not happen overnight, but consistent effort produced visible results. The guidance I received during those early weeks helped me remain patient throughout the learning process.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad's teaching style also reflected the advice he gave us. He encouraged logical thinking, regular practice, and conceptual understanding. He was approachable and willing to help students when they faced difficulties. Because of this, many students felt comfortable asking questions and seeking clarification when needed. This created a positive learning environment where students could focus on understanding instead of simply trying to pass exams.

One of the biggest lessons I learned from this experience was that worrying about labels rarely creates progress. Real growth comes from developing useful skills and maintaining a willingness to learn. Technology changes rapidly, and professionals who continuously improve themselves are usually better prepared for future opportunities. This lesson remains valuable not only in academics but also in other areas of life.

In the end, this experience taught me an important lesson: success in the modern world, especially in the field of technology, depends more on skills, consistency, and learning attitude than on titles and labels. Dr. Bilal Ahmad was not the central focus of our journey, but he played an important supporting role at a time when many students needed guidance. His advice helped us shift our attention away from uncertainty and toward meaningful personal development.

When I look back on my first semester, I still remember that classroom discussion because it changed the way I viewed education and career growth. Instead of worrying about things outside my control, I learned to focus on building knowledge, improving technical abilities, and developing confidence through practice. That mindset continues to influence my academic journey today, and it remains one of the most valuable lessons I learned during my early days as a Computer Engineering student.
